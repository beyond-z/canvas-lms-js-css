HOW TO ANSWER THE MOST COMMON QUESTIONS

Quick Question

Which of these questions are the top two most commonly asked in interviews? (Select two)

  • Tell me about yourself.
  • Why this job?
  • Tell me about a time you failed.
  • What are your greatest strengths?
  • What are your salary requirements
  • Tell me about a time you exemplified leadership.
  • Why are you leaving your current job

ANSWER

While "Tell me about yourself" and "Why this job?" are the top two questions, they are actually asking the same thing! At first glance, you might think, well no, those are two distinct questions. One is about me and one is about the job. Read Karen’s story to help explain why these questions, asked in an interview, are asking the same thing.

Story Time

Karen shook the hand of her interviewer and sat down at the table.

“It’s great to meet you!” said Karen.

“It’s great to meet you too,” said the interviewer. “Now why don’t you tell me a little bit about yourself.”

Karen paused, trying to hide her confusion. What did the interviewer want to know? There were so many things about herself she could tell…

“Well, sure!” Karen began. “I’m originally from Los Angeles. I have two brothers and a sister. My father died when I was little, so I barely remember him. I moved here for college, and while I planned to move back to LA after college, I recently decided to stay so I could move in with my boyfriend. So I’m now looking for

opportunities in this area that will help me grow professionally.”

The interviewer furrowed her forehead, took some notes, and then said, “Let’s move on to the next question, shall we?”

Quick Question

Why was Karen’s answer to this question not very good? (check all that apply)

  • The information she provides about herself is irrelevant to the job she is applying for.
  • It’s not focused
  • It gives the impression that she might prioritize relocation with her boyfriend over her job.
  • She says she’s looking for opportunities to grow professionally, but this is vague. What about this specific job
  • Her answer is too long
  • She’s not enthusiastic enough.
  • She’s not being polite.
  • She needs to provide more detail on her early childhood.

ANSWER

Karen made the rookie mistake of answering the question “Tell me about yourself” with an unfocused story about her personal life. Interviewers love to ask this question at the beginning of interviews to see if you’re able to focus when responding to such an unstructured question.

The secret to success is to respond like you would respond to the question, “Why this job?” You want to explain why you’re a good fit for this role at this company.

    What company is this candidate interviewing with? His answer is unspecific and doesn’t answer the question, Why this job? It’s also not helpful that he started off by listing all of his praises before explaining his passion for this kind of work.

  • I feel like I grew up in and out of hotels since my parents’ jobs were always cleaning rooms. I remember thinking how glamorous the hotel lobby felt and loved the hospitable atmosphere that the hotel employees created for their guests. The hotel felt like home to me; it felt safe.

  1. What brings you to the industry and relevant experience
  2. Your strengths and abilities
  3. Why this specific job

Stories are more convincing than Facts

Answering questions with stories is more compelling, memorable, and believable. Your responses to interview questions are really just stories, at the end of the day.

There are two storytelling frameworks you can use when answering interview questions: SYRCL and PAR. The point of using a framework is to keep your stories focused. The last thing you want to do is ramble on and never get to the point.

Using SYRCL

The first storytelling framework is called SYRCL (pronounced circle). Use this framework to answer behavioral questions, which aim to learn how you operate, learn, and deal with adversity. Some examples of these questions might be:

  • What is a recent accomplishment you are proud of and why?
  • When have you worked with a team and failed?
  • How would you work with a teammate who isn't producing great work? How do you motivate someone who doesn't have the skills, ability, or motivations that you have?
  • What would people say about your leadership style?
Quick Question

Match what each element of SYRCL should answer.

SITUATION Pick one situation you were in and describe it. Stay focused on getting the most out of that one situation and avoid sharing more than one example unless asked. You may confuse or dilute your message if you tell more than one.
YOUR ROLE What choices or decisions did you make? What did you do?
RESULTS What happened as a result of what you did? What were the outcomes?
CHALLENGES What challenges did you face? 
WHAT YOU'VE LEARNED What lessons did you learn along the way? What did you learn about yourself, your strengths, your skills and the value you bring?  

Quick Question

Here’s an example of a candidate’s SYRCL response to the question:
“How would you work with a teammate who isn’t producing great work?"

Read the response and then identify the situation, the student’s role, the result, the challenges, and what the student learned.

Last semester, I worked on a group project in my anthropology class where I was paired with two other classmates. We were required to choose a neighborhood, conduct fieldwork, and present an ethnography to the class. At the beginning of the project, our team split up the work evenly. However, my teammate who was in charge of creating the presentation slides waited until the last minute to complete his part, and the presentation left much to be desired in terms of professionalism. I knew our grade depended on this presentation, but I also didn't want to discount my teammate's work or redo the entire presentation. So I asked him if we could meet, and we worked together, slide by slide, to improve the presentation.

Because of our work, we ended up delivering an excellent presentation and received an A on the project. It was definitely a challenge to ensure my teammate felt valued when we were improving the presentation, but I tried to be empathetic and present it as a learning experience for both of us, and I think he appreciated that. I realized that I believe in the power of teamwork and that my authentic leadership shines as a patient, empathetic team player.

How this fits in

You’ll undoubtedly be asked in interviews about times you’ve failed, when you faced challenges, and about your greatest weaknesses. What the interviewer really wants to know is what you do in the face of adversity and what lessons you’ve learned.

To answer these kinds of questions, you can use the PAR framework (Problems, Actions, Results), just like Jerold did.

1. Identify the Problem.

2. Describe the Actions
you took to respond and resolve the challenge/failure/problem.

3. share what Resulted
from the action and your reflections on what you learned from it.

HOW TO ASK GOOD QUESTIONS (AND WHY)

Quick Question

What should an interview feel like?

  • A conversation
  • Pulling teeth
  • An interrogation
  • A quick chat
  • A test

An interview should feel like a conversation

That’s right — an interview is a two-way street. You might say something that sparks an idea or memory for the interviewer, and you will have time to ask questions too. The best interviewers feel like a seamless conversation.

At the end of the interview, your interviewer will undoubtedly ask, “Do you have any questions?” However, they're not really asking… In fact interviewers expect you to have questions, and they are often evaluating the quality of these questions to see if you did your research. You’ll need to come to the interview prepared with some excellent questions.

Quick Question

Which of these are good questions to ask the end of the interview? (check all that apply)

  • How much does this position pay?
  • How long after I join should I ask for a raise?
  • What were the biggest challenges you faced during your first year here?
  • What qualities do you value most in your junior team members?
  • What is the most important advice you give to new hires?
  • How do you think your company is doing based on the recent big change in its stock price?
  • What is your favorite part of working at X company?
  • What's your vacation policy?
  • What time do people usually leave the office?
  • When and how should I follow up after this interview?
  • How do you feel about the President's position on... ?

Some questions work better than others

You’ll notice that these good and bad questions fall into the following categories:

GOOD BAD
  • Simple
  • Genuine
  • Show your interest in the job
  • Builds a connection with the interviewer (professionally)
  • Political
  • Controversial
  • Company politics
  • Lifestyle (e.g. hours people work, salary) - Only ask these if you get an offer!
