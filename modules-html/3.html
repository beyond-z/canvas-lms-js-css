What are we talking about?

Quick Question

What would you guess are the top five factors that most people say make them happiest at work?

  • Salary and benefits
  • Having a good relationship with your manager
  • Opportunities to learn new things
  • Potential for promotion
  • Good work-life balance
  • Good fit between talents/skills and what the job requires
  • Good fit between company values and personal values
  • Working for a company/organization with good reputation
  • Perks like free lunch
  • Casual dress code
  • Job security
  • Lots of travel
  • Being appreciated for your work
  • Working for a financially stable company
  • Working in a cool office space
  • Working remotely (not in an office)
  • Working on interesting things (interesting job content)

Answer

According to a recent BCG survey# these are the top five factors that made Americans most satisfied at work:

  1. Working for a financially stable company
  2. Having a good relationship with your manager
  3. Good work-life balance
  4. Good fit between company values and personal values
  5. Being appreciated for your work

Looking at the survey results above, you'll notice that people who are happiest at work have jobs that align with their needs and with what they're good at; in other words, they are living their hustle.

 

 

Your hustle is the result of your heart – your values, passions, interests – and head – your strengths and talents, what you know – working in line with each other. You know that feeling when you are in the zone, losing track of time? If you can find work (for enough money) that makes you feel this way, that's your hustle.

Now that you've explored your heart, it's time to uncover what's in your head - your unique strengths and skills.

There are many ways to uncover your strengths. For example, as part of your Design Your Career project you'll be asking people who know you for stories that highlight your strengths. Another option is to take personality tests (such as Strengths Finder or Myers Briggs, which are also popular with recruiters).

Quick Question

You may have noticed that most of the strengths in the VIA test focus on interpersonal relations and character traits, as opposed to skills, knowledge, or specific talents. Why are those important to your career choice?

  • Employers sometimes use these tests to sort through job candidates, and it's useful to know what they'll be seeing about me
  • It always helps to know yourself
  • Some careers or jobs have more room to apply certain strengths than others, and knowing what those are can help me find a good fit.

Answer

All of the above are correct. When you encounter similar tests in the future it's usually helpful to try them out (but also keep in mind they're not all scientific, so take the results with a grain of salt).

Hey guys, it's Edgardo here from Braven, and I'm here to talk to you a little bit about my hustle. So, when I was in college, my dream career was to work managing public relations within the entertainment industry, and I was fortunate enough during my first job to land a role working at Nickelodeon. At first, I loved my job. I got to go to fun events and I was able to meet some celebrities and I also found myself starting to get really good at managing relationships, solving problems and thinking through them, as well as coming up with solution to day-to-day operations and so forth. One day I was working on this campaign and I learned about something called the achievement gap, which showed that people of color often from low-income backgrounds tend to perform on standardized tests at a much lower level than some of their peers. Not even that, that often, they would start two steps behind everyone else, so for me, at that moment, my heart plummeted because something that I saw growing up was not only a problem in my community, but was playing itself out nationwide.

So, I started volunteering because I wanted to do somethingabout the problem. I felt that at my role at work I was not able to make the kind of impact that I was hoping to make. After a couple of years of volunteering as a mentor, I started seeing my mentee make a lot of progress. And I started realizing that all the things I was good at in my full-time job — that I could actually bring them into a different field and help solve a problem that I was really passionate about and that really pulled on my heart strings.

After some time, I realized that I had a different career that was a calling for me. And that's when I really discovered my hustle — when I was able to bring together something I really cared about along with something I was passionate about. Now I focus on managing nonprofit programs so that students from all backgrounds have access to strong outcomes in their careers and education.
