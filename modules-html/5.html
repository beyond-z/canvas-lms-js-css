Networking is how you find a job, but it's something you do all the time, not only when you're looking for a job. And for some jobs it's actually part of the job when it comes to sales, fundraising, recruiting, or research.

Pulse check

How do you usually feel about networking?

  • I'm happy to network and I do it any chance I get.

    That's great. You can get even better through this module.

  • I'm relatively shy or I usually feel awkward about networking. It's kind of self-serving and arrogant, and I'm not that kind of person.

    Good networking skills let you bring your true self into the conversation, without need to "blow your own horn" and sell yourself too hard. We'll go through how to do it in this module and during Learning Lab.

  • I feel bad about asking people for favors or wasting their time on what I need/want.

    And what if those people need your help? People are always looking for good candidates to fill job openings, and they expect these people to come in through their networks. Remember the stat from earlier: five in six jobs are filled through networking.

Good networking means forming authentic relationships, which is what people do naturally.

The takeaway

People want to help others and talk about what they know best, which is what they've experienced personally.

That's basically what networking is.

There are many ways to meet new people.

Networking is a probabilities game. While you can't predict exactly where that new opportunity will come from, you will certainly improve your odds by expanding and diversifying your network.

While you could try to count on blind luck to randomly meet the right people, you can do it a lot faster through networking events.

General networking (events)

There are dozens of networking events happening every day in your area. Sometimes they are advertised as networking events; sometimes they are called meetups or panel discussions.

Ideally, you want to find events that relate to your career interests, as they will attract professionals in your desired field. To help you get started, here is a list of places to find networking events happening in your area:

  • Your university, its alumni association, and clubs all host networking events throughout the year. If you're unsure of how to find out about these events, stop by your career services office to get more information.

  • Check out your Braven Facebook group and the Opportunities Digest email, where the Braven community often posts information about local networking events. 

  • Meetup.com is a platform that hosts thousands of Meetup groups across the country that are tailored to different interests. Search for your location and interests to find an upcoming event.

  • Eventbrite is a quick way to browse and search for local events. Search for events using relevant search terms, choose one of their categories like "Business" or "Science & Tech," or choose the "Networking" event type.

  • LinkedIn Groups: Use LinkedIn Group's "Discover" capability to find relevant and local LinkedIn groups and join them. These groups will post any in-person events they are hosting.

What to do when you meet a new person at an event

How to get started

Quick Question

You're at a networking event. How would you approach this random group of people and strike up a conversation with them?

Come up with five things you could say:

Some openers that almost always work
The Classics

When in doubt, just try the basics: asking what someone does, inquiring why he or she is at the event, or even just reaching out your hand and saying hi.

  • "Hi, I don't know too many people here, so I wanted to introduce myself. I'm [name] and I work at [company]."
  • “So, what do you do?"
  • “So, what brought you here today?”

Basic things you have in common

No matter what, you've got at least a couple things in common with every person in the room: the event you're attending, the place it's being held at, and the food and drink you're consuming. Use that to your advantage!

  • "How did you hear about this event?"
  • "What a beautiful venue. Have you been here before?"
  • “How are you enjoying this event so far?”

The Newsworthy

This is another thing you have in common with just about everyone. Try to avoid veering into politics unless that's your industry.

  • “What do you think about [topic related to the event]?"
  • "Any chance you read the news today? I missed it, and I'm curious what's going on with [topic]."
  • “Did you catch the game last night?” (only ask this if you can actually talk about sports!)

Great for Introverts

If you’re an introvert, walking into a room full of unknown people can feel extra intimidating. One of our favorite approaches is to look toward the outskirts of the room and find someone who looks a little lonely. Maybe that person sitting alone doesn’t know anyone and is just hoping that someone will come talk to them. Be that person, and try one of these lines.

  • “Man, these networking events can be so crazy. Mind if I join you over here where it’s a little quieter? I’m [name], by the way”
  • "Since we're both here at the [buffet, bar, same table], I feel I should introduce myself. I'm [name]."
  • "I’m feeling pretty overwhelmed by the deluge of info that’s being firehosed at us today. Is there one nugget of brilliance that’s really resonating with you?"

The Totally Random

If all else fails, consider one of these:

  • "Any chance you know a great [sushi /Mexican / burger … ] place around here?"
  • "If there is one question you do not want me to ask you, because you are sick and tired of answering it, what question would that be?”
  • “I'm new to this. How do you usually start a conversation with someone you haven't met before?”
Quick Question

When you introduce yourself you're likely to experience a surge of adrenaline. That's totally normal, but it also means you will have a hard time taking in the other person's name.

Which of following is not a trick used by professionals to remember their conversation partner's name?

  • Repeat it a few times: "Nice to meet you, Sally" and later "so, Sally, what do you do?" and "it was great to meet you, Sally"
  • Ask them to repeat it: "I'm sorry I didn't catch your name, could you please repeat it?"
  • Think about someone famous or another word that sounds similar and try to mentally connect it with the person's face. For example, if you meet someone named John and he wears glasses, you might think of John Lennon of the Beatles.
  • Take a selfie with them

Answer

Remembering someone's name isn't really about memory, but about focusing your attention on that person. Most people can become very good at remembering names with just a bit of practice of techniques like the ones listed in the question above (except the selfie, though).

What to talk about

The other person

If there's one thing people can always talk about, it's themselves. Make things easy for yourself by asking them questions and then following up with more questions about things you find interesting. Remember you're talking to a human being, so try to be authentic and don't fake interest (it will show). If you feel the conversation isn't flowing very well, consider wrapping it up (see below).

Yourself

Sometimes the other person will ask you one of the classic questions: "So, What Do You Do? / Tell Me About Yourself / What Brings You Here?" Sometimes you won't be asked, but will still need to communicate quickly who you are and your interests.

That's the perfect time to launch into your elevator pitch: an extremely short introduction to help the other person know who you are and what you're interested in. It has to be so short that you could effectively present yourself (and/or your project/organization) on a short elevator ride, hence the term Elevator Pitch.

Make a list of skills and strengths you’ve developed from previous experiences and think about how they relate to what you want to do. Then, pick the most relevant ones to work into your elevator pitch.

How to wrap up nicely

Conversations are live things, and as they age they can lose momentum. Use your intuition and try to spot cues from the other person to see when it's time to wrap up (like their eyes wandering around the room).

Quick question

Which of the following is a good way to hint that the conversation is about to end?

  • I feel like I've taken a lot of your time…
  • This has been great but I see a few other people I need to say hello to…
  • I'm going to try to get a snack/drink before they run out…
  • Well, this has been a real pleasure…
  • Wow, look at the time!…
  • I really have to pee…
  • I apologize, I have to run home now to feed the cat…

Answer

The hint is usually followed by an ask, such as:

…but/and I'd love to stay in touch! Here's my card / can I have your card? / Can I find you on LinkedIn?

Make your network even stronger by always asking yourself: who do I know who might be a good match to make?

Quick question

Informational interviews are not just about asking the right questions. You're trying to be considerate of the other person's time, make a good impression, and keep the door open for further conversation.

What else should you be doing around your conversation? (check all that apply)

  • Prepare questions ahead of time
  • Take notes throughout the conversation
  • Make eye contact (if in-person or on video)
  • Actively listen (nod, repeat back what you heard)
  • Give your elevator pitch
  • Develop rapport
  • Thank the person for their time and insights
  • Send a thank-you email within 1-2 days

    This one is absolutely critical. See the next section and Productivity Corner to make sure you do it well.

  • Keep in touch after the interview

    See the next section and Productivity Corner for ideas on how to do this.

  • Spend most of the time talking

    You should drive the conversation, but the professional you're interviewing should be talking most of the time.

  • Ask if you can call them again tomorrow for more advice

    That's a bit extreme, but you should definitely ask if you can follow up.
    For example: "Thanks so much for meeting with me today. I'd love to stay in touch. And if it's possible, I'd love your feedback on my resume. Could I have you take a look?... Great! I'll send it over e-mail next week!"

  • Be sure to ask all the questions on your list

    You might not get to every question. It's important to stick to the time you agreed upon for the meeting (everyone's busy!). Also, go with the flow; a new question might come to mind as you're listening.

"The mark of a good conversationalist is not that you can talk a lot. The mark is that you can get others to talk a lot. Thus, good schmoozers are good listeners, not good talkers."

Guy Kawasaki

    Let's see how to ensure it doesn't happen again.

Thankfully, there are a lot of tools out there that can help you manage your network so that you don't make this kind of mistake. Here are three to consider:

Use a networking spreadsheet

Have a page for each company

This will help to keep each company isolated, and it will tip you off easily to companies that you have been ignoring.

Keep track of the following
  • The person's name
  • Their job title
  • Date of each contact
  • Brief notes of each contact (e.g. what you asked of them)
  • Date of planned follow-up
  • Did you follow up

When following up try giving them specific appreciation. It goes a long way because it shows you were paying attention. Something as simple as, "I especially enjoyed hearing your thoughts about the culture in the Denver plant" shows that you genuinely valued their time.

Track next steps

Make sure you are building your network intentionally and know your next steps with each contact — or at least each prioritized contact.

Set calendar reminders to follow up

Aside from putting in the times of your upcoming informational interviews, it would be a smart idea to schedule reminders to follow-up with quality contacts.

Maybe you are still a year away from applying and you don't want to wear out a contact. In this case, set a reminder three months from now to ask them to speak with you about a different topic. As you speak with more people, you will begin to develop more advanced questions that matter to you, so coming up with additional topics of discussion should be very doable.

Use an app

A CRM app such as Boomerang for Gmail can help you track follow-ups and even schedule emails to be sent when it's time to follow up with one of your contacts.
