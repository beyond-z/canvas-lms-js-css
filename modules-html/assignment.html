    Design Your Career Project

    Submit Assignment
    • Due No Due Date
    • Points 180
    • Submitting a text entry box or a file upload
    • File Types pdf

    People who are happy and successful in their careers pursue roles and organizations that align with their strengths and passions. As a Braven Fellow, you have the opportunity to design your career with this foundation in mind and set a vision for yourself that truly represents the unique individual you are.

    This project has three parts:

    Part 1: Strengths 
    Begin after Learning Lab 2: Lead Authentically     		With the help of friends, family, and colleagues, you will craft a Reflected Best-Self Portrait that will help you see when you are at your personal best.
    Part 2: personal Roadmap 
    Begin after Learning Lab 4: Discover Your Career Path     		You'll create a long-term and near-term plan to realize your vision for your career.
    Part 3: SLIDE DECK 
    Begin after Learning Lab 4: Discover Your Career Path     		You will prepare a slide deck to invest your Braven community in your vision for your career.

     

    • The 3 parts of this project are due No Due Date .
    • This project is worth 14% of your total grade.
    • To set yourself up for success, review the rubric before you begin and before you submit.

    PART 1: STRENGTHS

    The better you know yourself, the more likely you are to design a career that brings you happiness, the clearer you will be about decisions related to your career, and the easier it will be to advocate for yourself in the workplace.

    The Reflected Best Self Exercise is a multi-step process by which you begin to understand who you are at your best, based on feedback from other people. You will ask former classmates, employers, professors, friends and family members to give you short stories (anecdotes) about you to help you:

    • identify your personal strengths and how you add value to teams and organizations,
    • identify what kinds of situations bring out the best in you, and
    • create your personal and career development plans (personal roadmap).

    You will generate a portrait, or description, of your reflected best self, based upon the short stories you've collected. This structured feedback exercise will provide you with a rare opportunity to learn important attributes of your strengths, values, and your leadership that you may have never realized before.

    You will complete this exercise by following these steps:

    Step 1
    		 Identify 15 potential respondents
    Step 2
    		 Request reflected best-self stories
    Step 3
    		 Summarize key moments from best-self stories
    Step 4
    		 Analyze all best self stories and identify top 3 strengths
    Step 5
    		 Compose the reflected best-self portrait

    Step 1: Identify potential respondents

    To get started, review the contacts on your phone, in your email account, address book, or social networking sites. As you build your list of potential respondents, consider people who have seen you at your best and people who will give you their honest opinion.

    We've found that contacting about 15 people or more should help you meet the minimum requirement of 10 respondents (see attached grading rubric, below), since not everyone you ask will be able to help you in time for your deadline. Ideally, you will receive at least 30 stories, or three stories each from 10 respondents, of you at your best.

    Solicit stories from a mix of folks - family members, friends (old and recent), managers and classmates (former and current), and anyone who has had extended contact with you. When you create a list of potential respondents, double-check to make sure a cross-section of people from your spheres of influence is represented.

    Although it may feel uncomfortable, you should know that past participants have found that their respondents have been quite willing, even eager, to assist with this exercise!

    Step 2: Request reflected best-self stories

    Once you have a list of about 15 or more people, compose a story request and send it to them. While there are many ways to solicit and gather these stories (via email, phone call, or in-person), the below example is an email template you can use to reach out to a potential respondent. Regardless of which method you choose, we recommend personalizing each request from your users.

    Dear [name],

    I hope this message finds you well! I am writing to request your help with a project. I am taking a course on leadership development and career-acceleration as part of the Braven Accelerator. I would be grateful for your help with one of the required exercises for the course, the Reflected Best-Self Exercise. The purpose of this tool is to help me expand my understanding of who I am and what I do when I'm at my best so that I can choose a career that helps me leverage my unique strengths and assets.

    I am asking people who know me well to provide me with three stories of when I was at my best in their eyes, and what my positive contribution was in each story.

    These stories may describe my approach to people, challenges, tasks, or a work environment, and things that I say or do in critical times or everyday routines that make a difference. Please be sure to provide details so I can understand the context, what happened, and what my positive contribution was. I have included some examples below of what these stories could look like.

    Please send me your three stories by [insert date]. Thank you very much for your cooperation!

    [insert name]

     

    Story examples:
    1. From a work colleague: "You have the ability to get people to work together and give all they have to a task. For example, I think of the time that we were working on the Alpha project. We were getting behind and the stress was building. We started to close down and get very focused on just meeting our deadline. You noticed that we were not doing our best work and stopped the group to rethink our approach. You asked whether we wanted to just satisfy the requirements or whether we wanted to really do good and important work. You reminded us of what we were capable of do- ing and how each of us could contribute to a better outcome. No one else in that room would have thought to do that. As a result, we did meet the deadline and created a result we all feel proud of."
    2. From a friend: "You have capacity to persist in the face of adversity. For example, I think of the time that we were helping Lila empty her flooded basement. Her family lives far away and she was impossibly short-handed. Instead of getting overwhelmed with her, you became more focused than I have ever seen anyone get. I think you went 24 hours without sleep to help her remove the water. I was amazed that you could maintain a positive attitude and consistently helpful orientation to Lila under those conditions."
    3. From a boss: "You are great at building relationships critical to project success. For example, I think of the time that: We were working for a clothing company committed to using organically grown cotton, and to having fair labor practices. You were the liaison with the various agencies and individuals critical to sourcing the organic co on, and to creating the worker-owned sewing cooperatives here and in Central America. Despite resistance, you crafted and co-created a vision for how the business could work to meet these radically different goals. You easily navigated cultural differences and built strong relationships that thrive to this day."

    PART 2: PERSONAL ROADMAP 

    Fellows find it helpdul to PDF this entire project when they're done so they can continue to reference it throughout the semester and beyond! 

    Now that you've internalized your unique strengths, it's time to use this information to set your vision for your career. Start by zooming out to where you want to be 10 years from now before zooming back in to what you need to do in the near term to stay on track.

    career timeline

    A Backwards Plan is a tool professionals use to keep their eye on the goal. They start with the end result in mind and then work backwards to understand the steps they need to take to get there. When you implement the plan, you start at the beginning, but you'll know that each step you take will be en route to the final goal. 

    Complete a Backwards Plan here so that you have a vision for the path ahead of you. Feel free to use online resources as well as friends, advisors, and/or mentors to create a logical, ambitious, and realistic plan.

    Remember, it's completely normal to be unsure of what you want to do. You're bound to change course at some point. Think of this plan as set in sand rather than stone. For some Fellows, their plan will be more of a straight shot, and for others, it will be a windy path. Either route is completely fine. Keep your hustle in mind and try something out, as the exercise will help provide you with direction and guidance in the near-term.

    Still not sure where you want to be in 10 years? Think about where you'd like to be after college graduation. What kind of job would make you happy? Now, imagine a few years went by and you received a promotion, maybe two. What role would you be excited about then? How would you be fulfilling your hustle and playing to your strengths?

    college roadmap

    Now that you have an idea of your career path, it's time to think more near-term about the steps you're going to take to secure that first opportunity out of college. To help you, Braven has worked with top national companies to identify what college graduates most need to attain a career-accelerating job.

    Your goals and milestones should be SMART (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Realistic, and Time-Bound) and begin with verbs to ensure they're actionable. While big goals may be farther out, milestones are like markers along the way to your big goals:

    Essential elements to attain a career- accelerating job Where am I now? What have I already accomplished? big Goal - Where do I want to be by the time I graduate?* Milestone on the way to my big goal - Where do I want to be by the end of this semester?*
    Good GPA (>3.5)
    Professional exposure and internships
    Leadership and extracurricular activities
    Rigorous classes aligned to your career focus
    Adult Mentors
    Peers and support networks

     *If you are graduating this semester, you can think about this a bit differently. Keep your big goal as where you want to be by the time you graduate, whereas your milestones can be your goals for the semester's midpoint.

    PART 3: slide deck

    No matter where your career takes you, chances are you will be required to create slide decks to clearly and succinctly present information to others. "Decks" can be made with PowerPoint, Google Slides, Keynote, or a host of other applications, and they are prevalent because they can provide a visual and engaging medium for presenting and telling stories.

    Your task is to create a compelling slide deck for your LC, TA, and cohort to communicate what you plan to do in your career and why. You will not be presenting alongside this deck, so it needs to be able to stand on its own when your LC, TA, and cohort read it. You can decide the most effective way to portray this information, but be sure to include the following:

    • Your 10-year career timeline (here are some examples for inspiration)
    • Why you chose this path and how it relates to your strengths and passions
    • What you need to accomplish by the end of this semester and by the time you graduate
    • Your growth areas and how you will work on them this semester

    Your LC, TA, and cohort will be able to use the information you present in the deck to help hold you accountable to your goals and suggest ways to further your career. Use guidance from the module and the rubric to ensure you're creating an engaging, clear slide deck.

    Submit your deck as a PDF and attach it in an email to your LC and cohort.

    Ensure you have submitted your deck as a PDF file.
