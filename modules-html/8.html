Professionalism

Quick Question

What should you wear to a job interview?

  • What employees at the company wear to their more formal meetings

    This is a good gauge! If you’re in doubt, wear a suit, button-down shirt, dress pants, or conservative skirt/dress.

  • Jeans and a t-shirt because that’s what the team photos online show employees wearing

    Even if it’s a casual work environment, you want to err on the safe side and dress up a bit. In this situation, a button-down and nice pants would work.

  • That three-piece suit I wore to my sister’s wedding that is a bit too tight for me now

    A three piece suit it probably too dressed up for most interviews, especially entry level. You also don’t want to be uncomfortable in clothes that don’t fit perfectly — it might distract you during the interview.

  • My hot pink Hawaiian shirt with unicorns shooting laser beams

    More daring colors or prints and interesting accessories might work for a more creative company or position, but in general it might be smart to veer away from this. More muted colors will keep the focus on your conversation instead of what you’re wearing.

Unspoken Norms

What if it doesn't feel right?

Much of this guidance follows White normative culture as most companies in the this country operate by these norms.

Operating under dominant culture norms that aren't your own can feel strange. Try practicing thes following strategies, but only as long as you're feeling okay with it:

MAKE EYE CONTACT

Try to look people in the eyes when you're speaking to them. If you're responding to multiple people, try to create eye contact with each person for about one thought or sentence so everyone feels included.

SHAKE HANDS

A firm handshake upon meeting and when you leave is typically expected. Try to practice your handshake with a friend or mentor if you feel less than comfortable.

MIND YOUR POSTURE AND OPEN BODY LANGUAGE

Interviewers read your body language for cues about your disposition. They like to see you sit up straight and keep your shoulders open and relaxed (not hunched or tight). Avoid fidgeting with your hands or with objects such as a pen, your hair, or your clothes. This may be seen as insecure or distracting. Let your hands rest on the table or in your lap when you're not gesticulating.

SPEAK DIRECTLY AND CONFIDENTLY

As you respond to interview questions, think about framing and what you did rather than what the group did. White normative culture places a lot of value on the individual, and interviewers likely want to hear about your specific role in the scenario or challenge you're describing.

The reason employers interview candidates is proof that hiring is more of an art than a science. And just like art, the overall perception they get matters more than the details.

Whatever happens, let’s please keep in touch!


Regards,


Trina

How to ace phone and virtual interviews

Quick Question

What is the key difference between a phone/virtual interview and an in-person interview?

  • You can do the interview anywhere
  • You can wear sweatpants
  • You can cook dinner at the same time
  • It’s more difficult to make a personal connection with the interviewer

Answer

At first, you might think that phone or virtual interviews are easier than in-person interviews because you don’t have to travel to anyone’s office.

Alternatively, they can be more difficult, because you lose the chance to read body language, make eye contact, and really read the cues of the situation.

Nonetheless, there are specific strategies you can use to be as prepared as possible. After all, you will undoubtedly encounter both phone and virtual interviews in your job application process.

Virtual Interviews

Quick Question

 %

Answer

With 55% of employers using virtual interviews (and growing), chances are you will be invited to a virtual interview at some point.

Here are the nine winning strategies you need:

Top Three TECHNOLOGY strategies

Interviewers may be patient if you are having technical difficulties, but wasting interview time puts you at a disadvantage.

  1. Practice using some video conferencing tools beforehand, so that the interview is not your first time. Some commonly used services include:
    • Skype (requires download)
    • Google Hangouts (requires browser plugin installation)
    • Zoom.us
    • Attend.in
    • Join.me
    • Webex (paid service, but you can test drive it for free)
    • GoToMeeting / Citrix / LogMeIn (also paid, test drive here)
  2. Set your device right:
    • Use a laptop or a desktop computer. If you must use a tablet or phone, prop it up to avoid shaking and free up your hands.
    • Ensure your video camera, speakers, and microphone work.
    • Have headphones handy in case of echoing or other sound issues.
  3. Only use a consistently strong connection. If sign-on is required, do it 5-10 minutes early so that you can troubleshoot if necessary.
Top three PROFESSIONALISM strategies
  1. If using a video service you also use for personal needs, make sure your username and profile photos are professional, or create a separate account.
  2. Even though you're not in the same room as the interviewer, they can still see you. Wear the same outfit you would wear to an in-person interview, and find a distraction-free background in a quiet, clean space where you can be alone. Not a coffee shop, but definitely not a bathroom either.
  3. Defend yourself against any distractions by closing all windows and apps and disabling all notifications on your computer and phone (which should be on silent mode with no vibration).
Top three note-keeping strategies
  1. Have a handy copy (preferably printed) of any application documents you sent to this employer. They'll likely have a copy handy as well, and you may want to reference your relevant experiences and strengths.
  2. Have a list of questions you have brainstormed to ask at the end of this specific interview, or other talking points that might help you answer expected questions. However, make sure not to look like you're reading a script. Practice beforehand and use the notes as aids, not crutches.
  3. Have a notepad and pen/pencil ready so you don't have to type while on video chat. Use it to write down the names and titles of your interviewers, and any notes for follow-up.

Technical questions

Quick Question

If you were asked in an interview, “How many light bulbs are there on the island of Manhattan?” How would you answer?

  • Be honest and say, “I have no idea.”
  • Ask if I could Google it on my phone. Google has all the answers, after all.
  • Say, “The same number as the population of Manhattan.”
  • Walk through a logical response: “There are 10 million residences in Manhattan that use on average 10 light bulbs per residence. 10 times 10 million is 100 million. There are just as many businesses and street lamps. So that would total about 300 million.”

Answer

The point of these questions is for the interviewer to understand how you think. What really matters is showing your work as you go. Getting the right answer is not the point.

Pro Tip: Use round numbers so you can do the math easily.

It's also okay to ask questions along the way. It shows you can communicate and collaborate and not just blindly follow orders.
